# Trust, easily lost, never recovered  
  
**Money is an accounting entry**; a number that we trust is good for all debts, both public and private.  

**Money is trust**; trust in society that transacting is fair, transparent and equitable.  

**Money is highly regulated**; regulated through accounting, otherwise there can be no trust.  

The past few communications demonstrate that the internal APS accounting is poor.  When pressed, the APS goes silent, a disturbing sign.  

It is not the specific fault of the current group of managers; it is a ***structural defect***.  You see the APS runs multiple sets of &quot;books&quot;, legally.  

1. Town Meeting appropriation (~$136M)  
    - payroll (~$103M)  
    - health care (~$18M)  
    - debt service costs (~$15M)  
2. State and Fed (~$26M)  
    - Chapter 70 funding (~$20M)  
    - grants, circuit breaker, revolving funds (~$6M)  
3. AASP, Community Ed, others (~$4M)  

FinCom and their report to Town Meeting only provides minimal oversight on the first ledger, while the remaining ~$30M is &quot;off the books&quot;.  As an aside, I presented a per pupil expense using just the Town Meeting appropriations, adding in the other two revenue sources results in a per pupil expense of almost $28K.  

## Money is fungible  

Once money enters APS control, there is no reported distinction in how those revenues are co-mingled with the Town appropriation or funds from daycare and child care.  There is no reported distinction between teacher salaries funded by Chapter 70 funding and the Town Meeting appropriations.  
  
Not even [the smartest guys in the room](https://www.amazon.com/Smartest-Guys-Room-Amazing-Scandalous/dp/1591840538) could successfully manage such a high percentage of revenues and expenses &quot;off the books&quot;.  

***That is part of the APS &quot;structural defect&quot;.***  

## Money is Trust  

The first time, [I outed APS accounting, was in 2010](https://web.archive.org/web/20100909034051/http://truepersons.com/2010/08/24/1-8m-aps-school-budget-mistake/) when the APS &quot;kited&quot; $1.5M between fiscal years triggering a Powers&amp;Sullivan audit; a very revealing audit for anyone thinking an audit catches financial shenanigans.  From that point on, whether through subsequent overrides, fees for athletics or kindergarten, an impromptu special education one year &quot;revolving fund&quot;, every time a hard  look at any APS financial presentation one can feel the difficulty they have providing an honest accounting.  Even now, the [FY27 APS budget](https://4.files.edl.io/34d2/02/18/26/170952-ff8ad1d8-4b4a-4ee3-852e-e049b0de2a31.pdf), as it were, starts off with a *decrease* in property tax funding which just happens to be exactly the amount of increased Chapter 70 state aid!  That is one way to identify poor financial management, moving money between disparate accounts.
